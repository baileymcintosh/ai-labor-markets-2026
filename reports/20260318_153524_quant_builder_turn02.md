## A_5ade902a: Talent Mismatch — Broader Economic Impacts

**The Beveridge Curve has shifted outward by ~88–92% relative to the dot-com era**, which is the single most powerful structural metric in this dataset. Here's the quantification:

| Cycle | Unemployment | Vacancy Rate | Position on Curve |
|-------|-------------|--------------|-------------------|
| Dot-com trough (2002-Q2) | 6.0% | 2.4% | On pre-AI frontier |
| GFC trough (2009-Q4) | 10.0% | 1.8% | On frontier (recession) |
| Post-COVID tight (2022-Q1) | 3.5% | 7.0% | Far outside frontier |
| **AI-era 2025 (current)** | **4.3%** | **4.6%** | **Shifted outward vs. 2002** |

The 2025 reading shows unemployment at 4.3% — *lower* than dot-com levels — but vacancy rates at 4.6%, which is *92% higher* than the 2002 vacancy rate of 2.4%. In a fully efficient labor market, 4.3% unemployment would accompany ~2.5–3.0% vacancies. The excess vacancy rate above that equilibrium represents **structural skills mismatch** — employers cannot fill roles with available workers because the required skills are AI-era competencies.

The education unemployment differential further confirms the mismatch: the skills gap ratio (unemployment rate: less-than-HS workers vs. college graduates) is approximately **4.8x**, compared to historical 3.0–3.5x ranges. Workers displaced from routine cognitive roles (customer service, data entry, medical scribing, basic coding) face a skills wall between their current capabilities and the available AI-era openings.

**Healthcare demand spike**: The 1.8 million sustained healthcare job openings cannot be resolved by AI and represent an acute structural labor shortage — distinct from AI-related displacement. The federal workforce cuts make this worse: a significant share of DOGE-displaced workers hold administrative and policy backgrounds, not healthcare clinical credentials. Retraining timelines of 2–4 years for clinical roles create a multi-year mismatch window.
